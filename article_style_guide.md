# **Thoughts On 237 — Editorial Style Guide (v1.0)**

### Positioning

*Thoughts On 237* publishes clear, sober analysis of Cameroon’s political, economic, social, and historical realities.
The tone is **analytical, restrained, and evidence-aware**, inspired by publications such as the *Financial Times*, *The Guardian*, *The Washington Post*, *The Wall Street Journal*, and *Der Spiegel*.

---

## 1. Article Structure (Mandatory)

### **Headline**

* Informative, not clever
* States the subject and implied argument
* Avoid metaphors, slang, or rhetorical questions

**Preferred formats:**

* *Claim-based:* “Cameroon’s Growth Problem Is Institutional, Not Financial”
* *Theme: Subtheme:* “Decentralization: Why Power Still Resides in Yaoundé”
* *Event + Meaning:* “After the Cabinet Reshuffle, Continuity Prevails”

---

### **Standfirst / Dek (2–3 lines)**

* Summarizes the article’s core insight
* Explains *why this matters*
* Neutral, analytical tone

> Example:
> Cameroon’s latest reforms suggest progress on paper. In practice, institutional incentives and political caution continue to limit meaningful change.

---

### **Byline & Context Line**

Optional but recommended.

**Format options:**

* By *[Author]* — *Thoughts On 237 | Analysis*
* By *[Author]* — *Yaoundé / Douala*

---

## 2. Body Layout (Standard Model)

### **1. Lede**

* 1–2 short paragraphs
* Anchored in a current event, trend, or observation
* Establishes relevance immediately

---

### **2. Background & Context**

* Essential political, historical, or economic context
* Factual and concise
* No opinionated language

---

### **3. Analysis / Core Argument**

* Main section of the article
* One idea per paragraph
* May include **subheadings** (neutral, descriptive)

**Subheading style:**

* “Why fiscal discipline has failed to deliver growth”
* “The political logic behind policy inertia”

---

### **4. Implications**

* Explains consequences for:

  * Citizens
  * Institutions
  * Businesses
  * Stability or governance
* Answers: *So what?*

---

### **5. Conclusion**

* Measured, forward-looking
* Avoids advocacy or dramatic predictions
* Ends with clarity, not certainty

---

## 3. Tone & Voice

* Serious, calm, and confident
* Analytical rather than activist
* Critical without being polemical
* Assumes an intelligent, non-specialist reader

**Avoid:**

* Emotional language
* Moral grandstanding
* Social-media phrasing
* Excessive rhetorical questions

---

## 4. Formatting Rules

* Paragraphs: **2–4 lines maximum**
* Use **bold** sparingly (section headers only)
* *Italics* for foreign terms or subtle emphasis
* Bullet points only when listing facts or outcomes
* No emojis, excessive dividers, or decorative formatting

---

## 5. Language & Style Conventions

* British or international English (be consistent)
* Clear, direct sentences
* Explain acronyms on first use
* Numbers under ten written out; 10+ as numerals
* Titles capitalized sparingly (President, Minister only when formal)

---

## 6. Evidence & Attribution

* Reference data, reports, or public statements when relevant
* Hyperlink sources discreetly
* Avoid unnamed speculation

---

## 7. Signature Element (Optional but Consistent)

End each article with **one** of the following:

**In One Sentence:**

> Cameroon’s core challenge remains political incentives, not policy design.

**What to Watch:**

* Upcoming legislation
* Institutional shifts
* Budget releases or elections

---

## 8. Editorial Aim

Each article should leave the reader thinking:

> *“This helped me understand Cameroon more clearly.”*

Not:

> *“This told me what to think.”*
